Lesson 3 — 3D Perspective Projection

Three Coordinate Spaces
Going from a 3D world to pixels on screen involves three spaces:
  1. World space — where objects live (x, y, z in the game world)
  2. Camera space — coordinates relative to the camera (introduced in Lesson 4)
  3. Screen space — 2D pixel coordinates on the canvas
In this lesson the camera is fixed at the origin looking down +Z, so world space and camera space are identical. That keeps the math simple.
The Projection Formula
Perspective works because things farther away subtend a smaller angle. A planet at distance z looks 1/z as large as it would at distance 1.

screenX = (worldX / worldZ) × focalLength + canvasCenterX
screenY = (−worldY / worldZ) × focalLength + canvasCenterY

The focalLength (≈ canvas half-width / tan(halfFOV)) controls field of view. Larger = narrower view. We negate worldY because screen-Y increases downward. This is exactly what a projection matrix does in OpenGL/WebGL — just written out by hand.
Depth Sorting — The Painter's Algorithm
We can't draw planets in any order: a close planet must cover a far one. The simplest fix: sort by depth (Z) from farthest to nearest and draw in that order, so each new planet paints over anything behind it. This is called the "painter's algorithm." (Real engines use a depth buffer, but the painter's algorithm is perfect for spheres.)
Apparent Radius
A planet with world-radius R at depth z appears on screen as a circle with radius (R / z) × focalLength pixels. Same division-by-depth idea — farther away = smaller circle. We draw the planet using this apparent radius.
